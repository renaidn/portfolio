    NotParkour

    overview: a 2 player browser game done for the class on connected interactive applications taken in spring '22.

    my role: UX/UI designer, web developer

    tools: Adobe Illustrator, p5.js, sockets.io, node.js, express.js

    a bit of the background story

    For this project me and my friend teamed up to make a game inspired by many collaborative games we have played together (It Takes Two, A Way Out, Pico Park). The game we were looking to make was a two player game, where players would be required to work as a team to clear a level.

    project goal

    To create a multiplayer browser game that would encourage active collaboration and team play in an intertaining format.

    game concept

    Not Parkour is an arcade style collaborative 2 player game. Levels are structured in such way that players won't be able to complete the level on their own. Players can either create the room or join one, where they will be assigned a character at random. Each of the characters have their own special ability. Agash is a tree that jumps high. Kyzyl is a mushroom with a very bouncy hat that can be used as a trampoline by Agash. They join their forces to collect the keys and escape from the dark castle.

    Initial steps

    We have started from building a basic grid (map) to store the information about the placement of the game blocks. We then added a square (the player character), which moved with WASD and spacebar, added the gravity, and some collision functions. Collisions were a bit tricky to handle. It took a bunch of for loops and if statements. For some reason, lower boundary of the game blocks did not work and the user would go through them whenever they jumped below a block.

    why pixel art?

    For the design, we initially considered staying with the grid style adapted from our sketches. However, we have quickly realized that lack of character could potentially make it harder for the user to connect with their game character. We needed to come up with a user friendly design that would feel inviting and in a way traditional for web games. For this purpose, pixel art style felt like a great choice. It's deceivingly simple looking style makes its viewers believe that the game must be just as simple. Pixel art is pretty concise, yet detailed enough to give the game some character.

    designing every block

    I was fully in charge of the game design. To make each of the blocks needed for the game I have used Adobe Illustrator. This project marks my first attempt at pixel art. After some tries and errors, I have settled on 24*24 grid, which felt like I could add the just right amount of details to make block features recongizable, yet not oversaturated with details. I had fun with this step and found the process to be quite therapeutic.

    what makes a game?

    As we have finished working on level designs and the main game logic, we have moved onto an important question: "What makes a gaming experience?". We wanted to create an experience that would feel well-thought-out to the users. That required some careful research and planning on our end. After all, we only had a month to bring our game to life. After some consideration, we have identified pause screen, game server window, a waiting room, and appropriate sound effects to be important additions to the game. I have designed those elements and spaces, while Shyngys added necessary code for those elements to function properly. Adding sound to the game was important for me, because I believe that sound is a powerful tool. It is relatively simple but extremely effective way of making a piece feel more thoughtful and impactful. Incorporation of these aforementioned elements allowed the game to feel more full-fledged and finished.

    my takeaways

    Working on this project I was once again reminded of the importance of team-work and active communication. This and planning ahead have made this game possible! I have discovered the beauty of pixel art through this project and as you may have noticed from my portfolio carried this style into my personal identity. I am proud of our final product and am looking forward to creating more interactive web applications!

    ASRM

    overview: a membership website ordered by a government sponsored medical association of Kazakhstan.

    role in the team: UX/UI designer

    timeframe: 2 months (summer '22)

    apps used: Adobe XD, Figma

    Problem Statement

    The ASRM of Kazakhstan were in need of a space where they could share relevant news in the field, announce upcoming events, and document past events to connect the experts and build a sense of community.

    Project goal

    To create a membership website that would assist in building a connected community through active exchange of information.

    My role in the team

    I have been working as a UX designer alongside 2 front-end and 2 back-end developers. My role was to produce high fidelity wireframes for desktop and mobile versions of the website based on references and requirements provided by the organization. These were mostly guidlines regarding the information, functionality, and pages that were needed to be included in the website.

    demolition and cleanup

    I have joined the project almost a month after the project have started, so there already was a foundation of the website by the time I have joined. Since we were tight on the deadline, I have decided that the best decision would be building upon elements that already exist.

    I have worked on identifying the issues with existing design and focused on resolving them. The main issue was the lack of consistency in each elements individually and as a part of the bigger group. On the landing pages alone there were 16 different font sizes. In my wireframes I have specified all of the measurements and sticked to the 8 point grid and limited the font sizes to 3 max. This along has brought significant improvement to the website.

    decisions made, reasons given

    I have settled on the blue and white color palette with orange as an accent color. This has given the website a traditionally "medical" look that is recongizable at a first sight. I have tried making the website elements as easy to navigate as possible, because the target audience of the website ranges from 30 to 65 years old. From my personal experience, these age groups do experience difficulties navigating non-traditional websites. In this project, creative approach to element design, website layout, and user interactions would not be suitable for the target audience and the purposes of the website. Comfortable and intuitive are the adjectives that best describe the experience that I was looking to create.

    looking back and going forward

    Since I have joined the project later than others, I have missed out on initial communication with the client, which I personally find very important, and which could potentially expedite the design process as I would have a better understanding of client's wishes and wants. I have communicated closely with the developers, which helped me understand my designs and choices better. Overall,his project have taught me acting fast in situations of crisis and quickly adapting to the unexpected changes.

    CrayolaDraw

    overview: interactive drawing website featuring crayola colors done for the class on connected interactive applications taken in spring '22.

    tools: HTML, CSS, JavaScript, p5.js

    why crayola

    One of the requirements for this project was actively using the data fetched from a json file of my choice. I have chosen a list of crayola standard colors because I have always found them to have a nostalgic appeal, which makes them less intimidating and actually quite inviting to use. This has led me to my project proposal.

    motivation behind the idea

    I wanted to create a wholesome online drawing experience for those who can't use special professional drawing programs but want to start exploring digital drawing.

    Middle school me would have been happy to find a program of this kind, because my laptop wasn't powerful enough to support graphic softwares, but I had a deep interest in digital art. Even though online drawing websites frequently have a rather limited functionality, it was a great starting point for me to pursue my interest and familiarize myself with a tricky drawing-with-your-mouse experience.

    functionality included

    The website includes all the essentials for a beginner friendly first experience with digital drawing tools. I have kept the number of tools and icons minimal as to not overwhelm a first-time user.

    That being said, CrayolaDraw includes such functions, as:

    • Drawing on canvas using three different brushes;
    • Changing stroke size using a slider under the canvas;
    • Choosing the color of the stroke from the provided palette;
    • Copying hex of the currently used color;
    • Clearing the canvas;
    • Changing canvas orientation (portrait / landscape);
    • Changing the size of the canvas;
    • Saving canvas on your device as a jpg file;

    design changes

    As I have added all of the envisioned functionality to the page, I have realized that there are better ways of organizing and presenting those functions. I have worked on finding a balance between representing crayola colors and providing an uncompromised drawing experience. I have made sure that every element on display is self-explanatory and functional. While two icons have dropdown menus on hover (canvas size, canvas orientation), I have decided put out all of the brushes options directly on display for the user to be aware of all of the tools options present.

    CAIR

    overview: a winning logo identity for a university research center at NYU Abu Dhabi chosen out of 3 final proposals.

    tools: Adobe Illustrator, Adobe Photoshop

    starting point and general direction

    I decided to explore the human / robot relationships through this identity. Specifically, I wanted this design to communicate the unique connection, the incredible exchange of information, and the productivity of these relationships, while also remembering about the fundamental differences between the humans and the robots, all of this delivered through one visual identity.

    the centerpiece and the concept

    Setting this ambitious goal, my starting point was the imagery of a handshake. An almost universal gesture of positive and welcoming interaction. So incredibly overused, but at the same time so very fitting for our goals. At first, I was hesitant about using this gesture, however this hesitation was gone after I stumbled upon Michelangelo’s “creation of Adam”. The iconic fingers reaching for each other. We can almost see an electric spark glimmering there. Beautiful and very inspiring moment.

    approaching it as a logo designer

    I am not a painter nor a photographer. I am a logo designer, who have shapes and colors as our tools. And I gladly utilized these tools in my sketches. I have started from detailed ./images and reduced the amount of details till I was able to catch this beautiful moment of connection. The robot hand finally meets the human hand and at the intersection of the handshake something new is being created.

    accessibility question

    As the shapes in this design are abstract and minimal, the color scheme plays an essential role in highlighting the intersection of two hands and hence is key to the successful communication of its idea. For this reason, I found it important to address the design accessibility, specifically color blindness friendly palettes, in this logo design.     COSIMO

    overview: a logo proposal for a London based streetswear done for an immersive studio course in graphic design that combines practice and reflection through a project- based approach to branding.

    tools: Adobe Illustrator

    about the client

    Cosimo is a clothing brand by Cosimo Ippolito based in London with a DNA of Italian craftsmanship. The brand's motto positions it in a circular way, “stories beyond design, design beyond stories”. It takes pride in it's sewing technique, aguglia, which translates as the "garfish" or the "sea needle" from Italian language.

    design specifications to take into account

    The few concepts that I want my logo proposal to focus on are sewing and aguglia. I find these two to be the unique selling points integral tothe brand identity of Cosimo. I also need to keep in mind that Cosimo is a clothing brand, so the final design must look like clear and recognizable on the clothes.

    design direction and possible options

    Above you can see some of the possible design proposal for the logo. In all of my designs, I have used the long and sleek shape of the aguglia fish to make a letter ‘C’. I then played with the angles and the widths to create some variations of this design.

    As you can see, the two designs in the left column have an additional detail to make them resemble the needle eye. This way I am communicating both concepts: aguglia, and sewing.

    The two designs on their right, on the other hand, only communicate the aguglia fish and have the upper part of their edge split into two to make it look like its mouth. I don’t favor these two variations, because they are too abstract and resemble a snake more than a fish. On the good side, they made me think of brands like Lacoste, PUMA, and Polo Ralph Lauren. These clothing brands have various animals illustrated in their logos and I have realized that I can do the same. For some reason, I occasionally forget that minimalism and abstraction are not the only ways to go for in a design. With this in mind, I have came up with the image of detailed but not too much aguglia and presented it as the final logo proposal.

    final thoughts

    I actually am happy with the way this design turned out. I think it would have looked good as an embroidery and perhaps there are a way of transforming it into a seamless monogram. I would have explored more presentation options if we were given more time to actually work on and develop our proposal before presenting them.
